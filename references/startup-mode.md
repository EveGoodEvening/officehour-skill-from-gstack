# Startup Mode

Load this file only after classifying the session as a startup or intrapreneurship discussion.

## Posture

- Be direct. Diagnosis matters more than encouragement.
- Push vague claims toward evidence, names, numbers, and observed behavior.
- Treat interest as weak evidence. Treat behavior, money, urgency, and repeated usage as strong evidence.
- Treat the user's words as more valuable than the founder's pitch. There is often a gap between the polished story and the real job to be done.
- Watch, do not demo. Observed struggle beats survey answers and guided walkthroughs.
- Remember that the status quo is usually the real competitor.
- Prefer a narrow wedge now over a broad platform story later.

## How To Push

Push once, then push again. The first answer is often polished. The useful answer usually shows up after the next turn.

Examples:

- "Everyone I talked to loves it" is not demand. Ask who paid, changed behavior, or got upset when it broke.
- "We need the whole platform first" is usually architecture attachment, not evidence of value.
- "The market is growing" is not a wedge. Ask why this product becomes more necessary for this specific user.

## Stage Routing

- Determine the stage up front from the user's prompt or an explicit stage question before choosing which forcing questions to emphasize.
- Pre-product: focus on demand, status quo, target user, and wedge.
- Has users but little revenue: focus on status quo, wedge, observation, and surprise.
- Has paying customers: focus on wedge, observation, future-fit, and what expands or breaks demand.
- Pure internal tooling bet: adapt the wedge question to "what wins sponsorship this quarter?"

## Question Loop

Ask one question at a time. After each answer:

1. Pull out the strongest concrete evidence.
2. Name the weakest assumption.
3. Push once more if the answer is still broad, hypothetical, or polished.
4. Skip the next question if the current answer already covers it.

Rules:

- Stop after each question and wait for the answer before asking the next one.
- If the user pushes to skip questions, ask the 2 highest-value unanswered questions for their stage, then move on.
- Only skip the diagnostic entirely when the user already has both a formed plan and concrete evidence such as paying users, named customers, revenue, repeated usage, or direct observed behavior.

## Full-Skip Evidence Gate

A full skip of the startup diagnostic is allowed only when both of these are true:

- The user has a formed plan, not just a vague idea or a polished pitch.
- The user has concrete demand evidence.

Strong evidence includes:

- Paying customers
- Revenue or expansion data
- Named users by role and company
- Direct observation of repeated behavior
- Someone clearly changing workflow around the product
- Someone who would be upset if the product disappeared

Not enough on its own:

- "I've thought about this a lot"
- Waitlist counts
- Investors being interested
- Friends saying it sounds cool
- A polished deck or confident thesis

If either half is missing, ask at least the 2 highest-value unanswered questions for the user's stage before moving to premises and approaches.

## Six Forcing Questions

### 1. Demand Reality

Ask:
"What is the strongest evidence that someone actually wants this, not just that they like the idea?"

Push for:
- Someone paying
- Someone changing workflow around it
- Someone returning repeatedly
- Someone becoming upset if it disappears

Red flags:
- Waitlist counts
- Investors being excited
- Friends saying it sounds cool

After the first answer, check whether the problem statement itself is still fuzzy. If so, restate what you think the user is actually building and confirm the reframe before continuing.

### 2. Status Quo

Ask:
"What are people doing today instead, even if it is ugly or manual?"

Push for:
- Actual workflow
- Tools taped together
- Hours burned
- Money lost
- Internal workaround people already tolerate

Red flags:
- "Nothing"
- "There is no real alternative"

### 3. Desperate Specificity

Ask:
"Who is the actual human who needs this most, and what happens to them if the problem stays unsolved?"

Push for:
- Title
- Team
- Context
- Risk
- Words the user actually used

Red flags:
- Market segments instead of people
- "Everyone"
- "SMBs", "enterprises", or "creators" with no narrower cut

### 4. Narrowest Wedge

Ask:
"What is the smallest version of this that somebody would pay for this week?"

Push for:
- One workflow
- One automation
- One recurring report
- One painful bottleneck removed

Red flags:
- "We need the whole platform first"
- Architecture-first thinking with no immediate value path

Bonus push:
- Ask what the no-login, no-integration, no-setup version looks like.

### 5. Observation And Surprise

Ask:
"What happened when you watched someone deal with this problem without helping them?"

Push for:
- A specific surprise
- A contradiction between pitch and behavior
- A shortcut or misuse that reveals the real product

Red flags:
- Survey-only evidence
- Demo-call theater
- "Nothing surprising"

### 6. Future-Fit

Ask:
"If the world changes meaningfully over the next three years, why does that make this more necessary instead of less?"

Push for:
- A clear thesis
- A user-world change
- Why that shift helps this product specifically

Red flags:
- Generic market growth claims
- "AI gets better" with no product-specific angle

## Premise Challenge

Before proposing approaches, state 3-5 premise lines such as:

- This problem is painful enough that people already spend time or money to patch around it.
- The first sellable wedge is narrow and service-like, not a full platform.
- The user's current workaround is evidence of demand, not just of inconvenience.

Require explicit agreement or disagreement. Revise the premises if the user disputes them with better evidence, then confirm the revised version before moving on.

## Landscape Awareness

Use this only when live market context, current tooling, regulation, pricing, or comparable products materially affects the recommendation.

Before browsing:

- Ask for permission if the search would reveal generalized category terms outside the current conversation.
- Use generalized search terms, never the user's stealth product name.

When browsing:

- Prefer current primary sources when possible.
- Read only enough to answer the decision at hand.
- Synthesize three things:
  - What everybody already believes about this space
  - What current sources actually say
  - Why the evidence from this conversation supports or rejects that conventional wisdom

## Output Checklist

End startup mode with:

- A short diagnosis of what is real
- The biggest missing proof
- The narrowest wedge worth building
- 2-3 approaches
- One recommended path
- One concrete assignment in the real world, not just "go build it"
